Project Overview

The Iberian Peninsula, due to its geographical location, has long been considered a key region for studying one of the most significant events in human evolution: the replacement of Neanderthals by Homo sapiens. This transition is part of the broader “Late Quaternary Extinction,” a process involving the disappearance of large mammals due to climate change, environmental shifts, and the arrival of Homo sapiens. Situating Neanderthal extinction within this broader context of biodiversity loss allows for analysis of how ecosystems transformed over time and how early H. sapiens groups adapted to them. Within Iberia, the Cantabrian region is the first where Neanderthals disappeared, followed by the arrival of H. sapiens with Aurignacian culture after a brief demographic gap. In contrast, Neanderthals persisted around 5,000 years longer in the Mediterranean area, indicating a period of coexistence between the two species. The reasons behind the displacement of Neanderthals toward southern latitudes, as well as whether there was segregation between them and our species, remain open questions. To address these questions, it is essential to turn to the primary source of data in Prehistory: archaeological sites. Cueva Kaite, an archaeological site in the Ojo Guareña karst complex (Burgos, Spain), offers new insights into this transitional period. Located at the biogeographic boundary between the Cantabrian and northern Meseta regions, the cave sits at a natural crossroads that has historically facilitated human and ecological interactions. Its strategic location and paleoclimatic records (like stalagmites, being studied since 2004) make it an ideal site for studying social, cultural, and ecological dynamics during the Middle-to-Upper Paleolithic transition. Since 2023, excavations led by Ana Isabel Ortega and me have uncovered abundant faunal remains and lithic and bone tools. The stone industry reflects characteristics of Late Middle Paleolithic technologies. However, challenges remain due to the fragmentary nature of the archaeological record, which often results from overlapping human and carnivore occupations and natural post-depositional processes. To address these challenges, the Palarq Foundation is funding the project “Subsistence strategies of the last Neanderthal populations in Cueva Kaite – Burgos.” Three key analytical approaches are being employed: (1) radiocarbon dating for chronological precision, and (2) paleoproteomic analysis using ZooMS (Zooarchaeology by Mass Spectrometry) to identify highly fragmented faunal remains, and (3) sedimentary ancient DNA metabarcoding to reconstruct plant and mammal communities.

Current status and progress

  • All paleoproteomic (ZooMS), radiocarbon, and sedaDNA analyses have been completed, and preliminary results indicate that these biomolecular approaches represent a significant advancement in understanding the formation processes of the archaeological record. They greatly enhance the information obtained through lithic analysis, archaeozoology, and taphonomy. These methods are being further complemented by studies in palynology, micromorphology, and OSL dating. We plan to conduct one more excavation season in 2026 to expand the excavation area before proceeding with the full publication of this newly discovered archaeological site.
  • The next step will be to integrate these datasets into a comprehensive database of archaeological and paleontological sites that include chronometric determinations, human cultures, and species/fauna identification. Then, I will build and run in silico modelling approaches to test the project's hypotheses.
